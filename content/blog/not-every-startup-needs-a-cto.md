Not every *seed stage* startup needs a CTO - sometimes you really want a founding engineer. Over the past three years, I’ve learned just how hot of a take this really is. Through trial and error, I’ve helped half a dozen early stage startups build products and engineering teams. These companies were able to build without giving up fifty percent of their common stock and a board seat.

This article is a summary of the work I’ve done as a founding engineer to date. My goal is to walk through what a founding engineer is, how they are different from a technical co-founder, and when it makes sense to work with a founding engineer. Throughout the piece I may use the terms *technical co-founder* and *CTO* interchangeably.

## What is a founding engineer?

I’m defining a founding engineer as a software engineer who:

- works with a non-technical founder to build out a software product.
- takes less equity than a co-founder (common stock vesting over four years).
- takes less cash than a dev shop (usually a market rate or better salary).
- has a skillset more suited to moving fast at an early stage company vs. scaling at a late stage company.
- will be at your company for a shorter duration than a co-founder (several years vs. several decades/life).

## How is a founding engineer different from a technical co-founder?

A founding engineer sits in contrast with:

- a technical co-founder. A technical co-founder is a co-founder who will spend most of their time writing code. This is usually someone the founder(s) have worked with in the past and wants to continue working with. Compensation is weighed more heavily towards equity than cash.
- a dev shop. Dev shops traditionally deal in cash compensation. This can be a great option for shipping a simple MVP or brainstorming. These organizations are not personally invested enough in your success to build truly innovative pieces of tech. Depending on the technical challenges of the product, a dev shop could be a good option, but there is a reason some [hesitate to say that](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QAJQOXMM7ak).

A technical co-founder is like any other co-founder. They own a portion of the company and build the company with you. Founders can hold various roles such as CEO, CTO, or COO. In this case, a technical co-founder is likely synonymous with the title CTO.

Founders should not feel pressure to have a technical co-founder. If they don't know or can't find someone who they want to commit to building a company with for the next decade or more, they could decide to hire a founding engineer. Over time, that person *could* move into a CTO role or they could exit the company like any other employee. From talking about what I have been doing with friends over the past three years, there is a huge untapped network of developers who want to be a founding engineers.

## Why be a founding engineer?

A CTO might want to step into a founding engineer role because:

- It will be less stressful. Running a company is an all-encompassing pursuit if you are doing it right. You end up living and breathing your business, which leaves little time for other things like exercise, family, hobbies, or travel.
- It will pay the bills. Again, if you are doing it right, founders shouldn’t be paying themselves good salaries. After many years building a business that ultimately goes bust, it’s easy to get behind on financial obligations.
- Mental health. Stress from working 24/7 and stress from being financially constrained can really mess with you.

## Who would want to be a founding engineer?

After selling my first company, I was *exhausted*. All I wanted to do was build software without having to worry about doing outbound sales. *Starting* a company is a huge commitment if taken seriously, which is exactly why I decided against it in late 2021. At the same time, I didn’t really want to go back to big tech. Having worked at [Uber](https://uber.com) (pre- & post-IPO) and [Handshake](https://joinhandshake.com) (Series B-C), I loved how much faster things can move in pre-seed/seed land. At big tech, work *feels like work*.

There are a ton of solid engineers out there who fit this mold. This cohort is mostly former CTOs who know how to build quickly, don’t want to work in big tech, and *also* don’t want the stress of dealing with non-engineering-related operational tasks that come with starting a company. When I was faced with this challenge, I struggled to find my place. I started doing whatever random consulting work came my way. The first time I helped a founder raise five and a half million dollars over three months, I felt like I won the lottery. The second time one of my MVP’s turned into a multi-million dollar seed raise, I knew I was onto something. At the start, I assumed shorter-term engagements made the most sense. By slicing my time up monthly, I can help more companies!

😅

After trying that for a while, I discovered just how hard it is to help a company succeed if they constantly believe you have one foot out the door. I realized through experience that longer term engagements maximize chances of a company's success. Now, I only work with companies where I make it clear I’m in it for several years, not just several months. Traditional vesting cycles insulate the company from giving away too much equity to the wrong people. I’m willing to take a smaller chunk of equity in a larger number of companies so all my eggs aren’t in one basket.

---

At the end of the day, startups are risky. It is difficult to take the big risks necessary to build a billion dollar company without having something to fall back on. Being a founding engineer gives you a bunch of smaller equity stakes in a bunch of companies, which I and many others value.
