# Learning product honesty through logistics

Terusama was formative because logistics does not let a product hide behind a polished surface for long. If the model of the work is wrong, the breakdown shows up immediately in coordination, timing, and handoffs.

That environment sharpened a lot of the instincts I still rely on:

* software needs to reflect the real workflow, not the one that looks cleaner in a diagram
* exceptions are part of the product, not edge cases to wish away
* trust comes from clarity and follow-through more than from novelty

## What stayed useful later

Working in that context changed how I think about software quality. The most valuable systems were the ones that reduced operational drag without pretending the operation itself would become simple.

That point of view still shows up in later AI and internal-tool work. The question is usually not whether a system can impress once. It is whether people can keep using it when the day gets messy.
