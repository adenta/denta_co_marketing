There is a predictable moment in a lot of AI projects when the first version works well enough to impress people in a room. A model responds quickly, a workflow gets compressed, and the conversation turns from curiosity to possibility.

That moment is useful, but it is also where a lot of bad product decisions begin. The demo is often the cleanest the system will ever feel. It usually has ideal inputs, generous assumptions, and no real organizational drag pressing on it yet.

What survives after that is usually much simpler: clear handoffs, understandable failure modes, a narrow surface area, and enough product judgment to avoid automating the wrong thing. Teams do not just need intelligence. They need software they can trust on an ordinary Wednesday.

That trust comes from the boring parts. Logging. Review loops. Escape hatches. Sensible defaults. Good interfaces around uncertain systems. The most successful AI products I have seen do not pretend uncertainty disappeared. They make it legible and manageable.

That is usually the line I care about most: not whether a system can astonish people once, but whether it can keep being useful after novelty wears off.
