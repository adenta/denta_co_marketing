# Attention does not equal intent

Most practice websites are built as brochures when they should behave more like a front desk.

Patients who are ready to book are not looking for a brand manifesto. They are trying to answer a narrow set of questions:

* Do you do the treatment I need?
* Are you close enough?
* Can I trust this office?
* How do I book right now?

If any of those answers are fuzzy, the lead leaks.

## Where the leak usually happens

The most common failure mode is not traffic. It is conversion friction after the click.

| Stage | What the patient wants | What weak sites do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Search result | Quick confidence | Generic titles and vague copy |
| Landing page | Clear relevance | Long hero sections with no decision path |
| Consideration | Proof and specifics | Thin service pages and weak trust signals |
| Booking | Low-friction next step | Hide the phone number and bury the form |

## Fix the first five seconds

Your homepage and service pages should immediately answer:

> who this is for, what you do, where you are, and what to do next.

That is usually enough to lift qualified conversion before you touch ads, SEO, or automation.

```rb
# Simple internal rule for page reviews
return :rewrite if hero_copy.words > 35 && primary_cta.above_the_fold? == false
```

## What good looks like

1. Strong page titles tied to actual treatments.
2. A visible primary call to action above the fold.
3. Real evidence: photos, reviews, insurance/payment clarity, and location details.
4. Service pages that speak to patient intent, not generic dental jargon.

The bar is not design novelty. The bar is clarity at the moment of decision.
