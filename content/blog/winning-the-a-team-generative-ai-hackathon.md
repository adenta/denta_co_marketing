*May 11, 2023, 9:00 AM ET*

This past weekend, I teamed up with Niraj Jayant, Alexander Whedon, and Saulo Bomfim for A.Team's first Generative AI Hackathon. We ended up winning, and I have been thinking a lot about why the project worked.

We built JAY, an intelligent claims assistant for the auto insurance industry. The core idea was simple: use AI to make claims intake faster, more consistent, and less painful for everyone involved.

What made the weekend interesting was not just that we shipped a prototype in two days. It was that the time pressure forced clarity. We did not have room for a broad platform story or a pile of speculative features. We had to pick a painful workflow, understand it quickly, and build the shortest path to something useful.

That is where the project became compelling. With guidance from Anu George, we focused on a part of insurance where speed, structure, and customer experience all matter at once. Claims is one of those workflows where small improvements compound fast. If you collect the right information in the right order and help people move forward with less friction, the value is obvious.

JAY was designed around that principle. It could handle voice or text, support multiple languages, and follow a structured conversation instead of behaving like a generic chatbot. That mattered more to me than any flashy demo. In enterprise software, reliability is usually more valuable than novelty.

I am excited that we won. I am more excited by what the weekend reinforced for me: the best AI products are not magic tricks. They are focused systems that reduce friction in real workflows so people can spend more time on judgment, creativity, and higher-leverage work.

I am also grateful for the team. This kind of project only works when the group can move quickly without losing the thread. Everyone brought a different strength, and the end result felt more coherent because of it.

If you want the outside view, A.Team shared the announcement on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ateams_were-thrilled-to-congratulate-andrew-denta-activity-7062834612862869504-Uu1C/) and published a longer recap on [how the prototype came together](https://www.a.team/insights/ai-prototype-hackathon).

For me, the biggest takeaway is simple. AI gets interesting when it stops performing intelligence and starts helping real work move.
